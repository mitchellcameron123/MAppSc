Singleton et al. (2019) finds that in the southwest United States, forest fires have become more frequent, larger, and more severe. Wildfires release pollutants, including fine particulate matter known as PM2.5. Exposure to PM2.5 from wildfires is linked to respiratory and cardiovascular health issues. Feng et al. (2016) finds that inhalation of PM2.5 can trigger lung inflammation, worsen asthma, and increase the risk of cardiovascular diseases, stroke, and premature mortality.The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the interaction between PM2.5 pollution and respiratory infections. Wu et al. (2020) found that a \(1\mu g/m^3\) increase in long-term average PM2.5 concentration is associated with an 11% increase in COVID-19 mortality. Given the health and environmental impacts of PM2.5 pollution from wildfires, intensified research efforts are essential.

However, causal forests must maintain honesty as required by (Wager2018?) so the bootstrapped sub-sample is further divided into a training set and an estimation set to meet the honesty criterion. Each tree is then trained and estimated using these two divisions of the bootstrapped sample similarly to a single causal tree. Honesty is essential in the causal forest process because it ensures each tree’s validity, leading to an asymptotically valid causal forest. While the causal tree is only able to use a proportion of the observations for estimation, the forest randomises the honest splitting in each bootstrapped sample and so each observation is likely to appear in both training and estimation samples across the whole forest.
